About me

I am a third year PhD student in the University of St Andrews. My main topic is looking at how galaxies evolve across time! This evolution includes how and why some galaxies stop forming stars, some gradually but others very suddenly, or how some galaxies suddenly go through a starburst (much more star-formation than normal). My study aims to unlock these question marks through modelling the stellar populations in galaxies to match their observed spectra, in a population all at once. I work with Prof Vivienne Wild.

Research topics

Post-starburst galaxies

Since the advent of the first large-scale galaxy surveys such as the 2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey, we have found galaxies tend to fall into two main types: blue, star-forming, relatively low mass spiral galaxies and red, dead, more massive elliptical galaxies. How did the star-forming spirals turn into the ellipticals? The most likely answer is the shutting-down of their star-formation, i.e. quenching.

Post-starbursts are galaxies found at an important stage of their quenching process, where they have recently gone through increased star-formation (the starburst), but then quickly followed by a rapid shut-down. My work reconstructs the star-formation history and chemical evolution history of this peculiar population from IFU surveys such as MaNGA, which then tells us about how their quenching happened and what processes drove them. The usage of IFU data also allows for spatially resolved study of the galaxy's evolution history too.

Jellyfish galaxies

Never expected to find jellyfishes in space? These odd-looking galaxies are satellite galaxies of large clusters that are falling into or passing through the dense cluster medium. The gas within the jellyfish galaxies are stripped away in a process called ram-pressure stripping, leaving behind tails that sometimes clusters of new star-formation can be seen within. My work focuses on connecting post-starburst regions in jellyfish galaxies with their stripping direction and mechanisms, understanding how much ram-pressure stripping and dense cluster environments in general contribute to galaxy quenching.
